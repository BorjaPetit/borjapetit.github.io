WORKING PAPERS
Intergenerational effects of child-related tax benefits in the US

Job Market Paper, January 2019.

Download: working paper, slides

Abstract: The presence of children in US households reduces tax liabilities through deductions and tax credits. Through the lens of the quantity-quality trade-off, these benefits distort parental choices over the number and human capital of children by altering their relative implicit price. This paper quantifies the effects of child-related tax benefits on fertility and intergenerational mobility using a general equilibrium life-cycle model with endogenous fertility choices and parental investments in children's human capital, calibrated to US data. I show that tax benefits increase fertility by 16%, but they do so at the expense of lowering human capital of children. More importantly, these effects are particularly strong among low educated mothers, which widens the gap in human capital between children of low and high educated mothers. As a result, the intergenerational persistence of education increases by 37% when tax benefits are introduced. I also show that education subsidies are also effective at fostering fertility but, as opposed to tax benefits, they do not decrease children's human capital, nor intergenerational mobility.

Monetary policy implications of state-dependent prices and wages  (Submitted)

Joint work with James Costain and Anton Nakov.

CEPR Discussion Paper #13398, December 2018.

Download: CEPR DP, working paper, slides.

Abstract: This paper studies the dynamic general equilibrium effects of monetary shocks in a “control cost” model of state-dependent retail price adjustment and state-dependent wage adjustment. Suppliers of retail goods and of labor are both monopolistic competitors that face idiosyncratic productivity shocks and nominal rigidities. Stickiness arises because precise choice is costly: decision-makers tolerate errors both in the timing of adjustments, and in the new level at which the price or wage is set, because making these choices with perfect precision would be excessively costly. The model is calibrated to microdata on the size and frequency of price and wage changes. We find that the impact multiplier of a money growth shock on consumption and labor in our calibrated state-dependent model is similar to that in a Calvo model with the same adjustment frequencies, though the response is less persistent than it would be under the Calvo mechanism. Wage rigidity accounts for most of the nonneutrality that occurs in a model where both prices and wages are sticky; hence, a model with both rigidities produces substantially larger real effects of monetary shocks than does a model with sticky prices only. We find that the state-dependence of nominal rigidity strongly decreases the slope of the Phillips curve as trend inflation declines. This result is not driven by downward wage rigidity; adjustment costs are symmetric in our model. Here, instead, price- and wage-setters prefer to adjust less frequently when trend inflation is low, making short-run inflation less reactive to shocks.

WORK IN PROGRESS
Productivity losses from firing costs with endogenous firm productivity growth.

Abstract: This paper quantifies the aggregate productivity losses associated with firing costs in a firm dynamics with endogenous firm productivity growth through innovation. Innovation makes the firm productivity distribution endogenous, and thus, potentially affected by distortions allowing the model to capture both the static and dynamic effects of firing costs. I calibrate the model parameters to match some key features of the Spanish and use it to evaluate the quantitative impact of firing cost aggregate productivity and output.

Endogenous firm productivity dynamics

Joint work with Juan Carlos Ruiz-Garcia.

Abstract: The literature on firm dynamics usually assumes that firm productivity follows an AR(1) process, and thus, productivity dynamics are exogenous. There are two reasons that motivate departing from this assumption. First, the AR(1) assumption implies that productivity persistence and uncertainty are constant across firms, which is not supported by the data. Second, the AR(1) assumption implies that productivity dynamics are unaffected by policies and frictions. In this paper, we provide a simple way to endogenize the dynamics of firms' productivity, adopting a control-cost approach widely used in the rational inattention literature. Our methodology does not add computational cost to a standard firm dynamics model, and therefore, it allows researchers to account for the effects of policies and frictions on the dynamics of firm productivity without increasing model complexity.

Intergenerational welfare dependence

Joint work with Javier López Segovia.

Abstract: US data shows a strong integenerational correlation in welfare participation. Existing evidence shows that both intergenerational transmission of economic status and welfare culture (stigma cost) are important in accounting for such correlation. In this paper, we propose a life-cycle model with incomplete markets to disentangle the relative importance of these two sources of intergenerational persistence in welfare programs participation. Additionally, we evaluate the welfare and intergenerational effects of different reforms in the presence of intergenerational transmission of welfare culture.
